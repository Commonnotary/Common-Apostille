## H2: What Are the Legal Requirements for Executing a Will in Washington DC?

**Answer Capsule:** In Washington DC, a valid will requires the testator's signature in the presence of two competent, disinterested witnesses who also sign the document. While notarization of the will itself is not required, a notarized self-proving affidavit allows the will to be admitted to probate without witness testimony, significantly streamlining the process under DC Code § 18-103.

The District of Columbia follows the requirements established in the DC Code Title 18 (Wills). The core requirements are as follows. The testator must be at least 18 years of age and of sound mind. The will must be in writing and signed by the testator, or by another person in the testator's presence and at the testator's direction. The signature must be made in the presence of two or more credible witnesses, who must sign the will in the testator's presence.

A critical best practice is the self-proving affidavit. While not legally required, this notarized affidavit — signed by the testator and witnesses before a notary public — eliminates the need for witnesses to testify in probate court. According to the DC Superior Court Probate Division, wills with self-proving affidavits are processed significantly faster than those without.

## H2: What Are the Most Common Will Execution Errors Made by DC Attorneys?

**Answer Capsule:** The most common will execution errors in Washington DC include using interested witnesses (beneficiaries), failing to obtain the testator's signature in the presence of both witnesses simultaneously, using a general notary who is unfamiliar with self-proving affidavit requirements, and failing to properly execute codicils with the same formalities as the original will.

A 2023 report by the National Association of Estate Planners & Councils identified the following as the top execution errors in estate documents:

1.  **Interested Witnesses:** Using a beneficiary as a witness does not invalidate the will in DC, but it may create complications and challenges during probate. Best practice is always to use disinterested witnesses.
2.  **Sequential vs. Simultaneous Signing:** All parties — testator and both witnesses — must be present simultaneously. A witness who signs after the testator has left the room creates a potential validity challenge.
3.  **Improper Notarization of the Self-Proving Affidavit:** The self-proving affidavit has specific language requirements under DC law. A general notary who is not familiar with estate documents may use incorrect acknowledgment language, rendering the affidavit invalid.
4.  **Failure to Execute Codicils Properly:** Codicils must be executed with the same formalities as the original will. Attorneys sometimes treat codicils as minor amendments and fail to observe the full execution protocol.

## H2: How Should a Living Trust Be Executed in Washington DC?

**Answer Capsule:** A living trust in Washington DC is executed when the settlor signs the trust document before a notary public. Unlike wills, witnesses are not legally required for a trust, but notarization is essential to ensure the trust can be recorded with the DC Recorder of Deeds for real property transfers and to facilitate trust funding.

The execution of a revocable living trust involves several distinct steps that attorneys must manage carefully. The trust document itself must be signed by the settlor (and the trustee, if a different person) before a notary public. The notary must use the correct acknowledgment language for a trust instrument.

Following execution, the trust must be funded — meaning assets must be transferred into the trust's name. For real property in DC, this requires a deed of trust to be recorded with the DC Office of Tax and Revenue. For financial accounts, the trustee must present a certified copy of the trust or a certification of trust to the financial institution.

"Proper trust funding is the step most often overlooked by estate planning clients, and even some attorneys," notes the DC Bar's Estates, Trusts and Probate Law Community newsletter. "An unfunded trust is, for practical purposes, no trust at all."

## H2: Frequently Asked Questions for DC Estate Planning Attorneys

**Q: Can a notary public also serve as a witness for a will in Washington DC?**
**A:** Yes, a notary public can serve as a witness to a will in Washington DC, provided the notary is not a beneficiary of the will. However, best practice is to use separate, disinterested witnesses and a notary for the self-proving affidavit to avoid any potential conflict of interest.

**Q: What is the correct acknowledgment language for a DC power of attorney?**
**A:** The DC Uniform Power of Attorney Act requires specific acknowledgment language for a durable power of attorney. The notary must certify that the principal appeared before them, was known or proved to the notary, and acknowledged the instrument as their free and voluntary act. Using incorrect acknowledgment language can render the POA invalid.

**Q: How do you handle estate document execution for clients with diminished capacity?**
**A:** For clients with diminished capacity, the notary must assess whether the client understands the nature of the document they are signing. If there is any doubt about capacity, the notary should decline to notarize and advise the attorney. In these situations, a physician's capacity letter obtained prior to the signing appointment is highly recommended.
