This guide will walk you through the steps to get an apostille for your Virginia-issued documents.

**Step 1: Ensure Your Document is a Virginia Public Document**

The first step is to confirm that your document is a public document issued by the Commonwealth of Virginia. This includes documents such as:

*   Birth certificates
*   Marriage certificates
*   Death certificates
*   Divorce decrees
*   Notarized documents
*   Corporate documents filed with the Virginia State Corporation Commission

**Step 2: Have Your Document Notarized (If Necessary)**

If your document is not a public record but is a private document, such as a power of attorney or a sworn statement, it must be notarized by a Virginia notary public before it can be apostilled. The notary’s signature is what the Virginia Secretary of the Commonwealth will authenticate.

**Step 3: Submit Your Document to the Virginia Secretary of the Commonwealth**

The Virginia Secretary of the Commonwealth is the only authority that can issue an apostille for Virginia-issued documents. You can submit your document by mail or in person. You will need to include the following:

*   The original document to be apostilled

### Apostille vs Embassy Legalization — What's the Difference and Which Do You Need

**Headline:** Apostille vs. Embassy Legalization: What’s the Difference and Which Do You Need?

**Body:**

When you need to use a public document in a foreign country, you will likely be asked to have it “authenticated” or “legalized.” The two most common methods for this are the apostille and embassy legalization. While they serve a similar purpose, they are not interchangeable. Understanding the difference is crucial to ensuring your documents are accepted abroad.

**The Hague Apostille Convention**

The key difference between an apostille and embassy legalization lies in whether the destination country is a member of the Hague Apostille Convention. The Convention, created in 1961, simplified the authentication process by creating a single, standardized form of certification: the apostille.

If your document is destined for one of the 118 countries that are party to the Hague Convention, you will need an apostille. The apostille is issued by a designated authority in the country where the document was issued (in the U.S., this is typically the Secretary of State).

**Embassy Legalization**

If the destination country is **not** a member of the Hague Convention, you will need to go through the process of embassy legalization. This is a more complex, multi-step process that involves:

1.  **Certification** by the Secretary of State where the document was issued.
2.  **Authentication** by the U.S. Department of State in Washington D.C.
3.  **Legalization** by the embassy or consulate of the destination country.

**Which Process Do You Need?**

To determine whether you need an apostille or embassy legalization, you simply need to know if the destination country is a member of the Hague Apostille Convention. You can find a complete list of member countries on the Hague Conference on Private International Law website.
